\section*{Preface}\label{sec:preface}
This study was conducted to explore and compare the financial accounting standards applied at universities in Switzerland and Mexico. Using the International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS) applied at ETH Zurich as a reference, the study aims to provide a clearer understanding of how these standards differ from the widely applied NIF standards in Mexico in terms of complexity and scale. Partnering with the Tecnológico de Monterrey in Mexico (ITESM), this project offered a unique opportunity to analyze the accounting practices of two distinct academic and cultural environments.

In order to gain an overview of the accounting standards applied in both countries, the table below has been created with the support of Prof. Dr. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo (Associate Professor of Accounting \& Finance, ITESM). The table shows the accounting standards applied, broken down by country, private or public sector, size and whether they are listed on the stock exchange.

The comparison shows that large and international listed companies in both countries primarily apply IFRS and in some cases also US GAAP. In contrast, different accounting standards are applied when medium-sized and small private companies are compared: In Mexico, the Normas de Información Financiera (NIF) are applied here and in Switzerland mainly the Swiss GAAP FER.

Looking at the public sector, it can be observed that different accounting standards are applied in Switzerland: First, IPSAS and the rule-based HRM2 accounting model derived from it, then Swiss GAAP FER, which is primarily applied by cantonal universities and universities of applied sciences, and finally IFRS for state-owned institutions such as Swiss Post.

Mexico, on the other hand, relies more on national accounting standards and regulations in the public sector like NIF and the Ley General de Contabilidad Gubernamental (LGCG). Like Switzerland, Mexico also has some state-owned companies that apply IFRS. It can be assumed that this is mainly for reasons of capital raising.

A special case is UNAM, which has NIF-based accounting standards but has an autonomous board within the university that defines the applicable accounting standards (for more details see \vref{subsubsec:evaluation_of_the_accounting_standards_applied_by_universities_in_mexico}).

\subsection{Accounting standards applied by universities in both countries}\label{subsec:accounting_standards_applied_by_universities_in_both_countries}
The next two sections list the accounting standards in the two countries derived from the overview (see \vref{subsec:overview_of_the_accounting_standards_applied_in_both_countries}) and then identify the subset of these that are applied at universities.

\subsubsection{Evaluation of the accounting standards applied by universities in Switzerland}\label{subsubsec:evaluation_of_the_accounting_standards_applied_by_universities_in_switzerland}
Based on the overview, accounting standards applied in Switzerland are listed below:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Swiss Code of Obligations (OR)\\
Scope: Required for all Swiss-based companies as a minimal standard.\\
Key Features: Basic bookkeeping and financial reporting, suitable for small companies.

    \item Swiss GAAP FER\\
Scope: For mid-sized companies, foundations, and non-profits.\\
Key Features: Modular, Swiss-specific, transparent but less globally accepted.

    \item International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS)\\
Scope: Large and publicly traded companies with international exposure.\\
Key Features: Principles-based, detailed, globally recognized, investor-friendly.

    \item US Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (US GAAP) \\
Scope: Companies mainly listed on US exchanges or with strong ties to the US.\\
Key Features: Rules-based, rigorous, detailed, primarily US-focused.

    \item International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS)\\
Scope: Public institutions.\\
Key Features: Principles-based, public-sector adaptation of IFRS for transparency and comparability.

\end{itemize}

\textbf{Which accounting standards are used by universities in Switzerland?}\\
The analysis, which was carried out across various universities and universities of applied sciences, showed that the following two accounting standards are used in accounting in institutions of the tertiary education sector:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item IPSAS\\
At the universities and research institutes at federal level, i.e. the institutions of the ETH Domain.

    \item Swiss GAAP FER\\
Mostly at cantonal universities and universities of applied sciences.

\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Evaluation of the accounting standards applied by universities in Mexico}\label{subsubsec:evaluation_of_the_accounting_standards_applied_by_universities_in_mexico}
As with the analysis of accounting standards applied by universities in Switzerland, we will first list all accounting standards in Mexico that apply to all types of legal entities in Mexico.

Based on interviews with Prof. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo in Mexico, the following accounting standards are relevant for legal entities:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) with the same scope like in Switzerland \\
(for a brief description see \vref{subsubsec:evaluation_of_the_accounting_standards_applied_by_universities_in_switzerland})

    \item US Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (US GAAP)\\
(for a brief description see \textit{dito})

    \item Normas de Información Financiera (NIF)\\
Legal entities in Mexico must generally prepare their accounts in accordance with the NIF, unless they meet the requirements for other specific accounting standards such as IFRS or US GAAP. The NIF is the primary accounting standard in Mexico and is mandatory for most companies.\\
Simplifications are only possible for business operations under the \textit{Régimen de Incorporación Fiscal (RIF)}, but these can only be claimed by \textit{personas fisicas} (natural persons) who operate as sole traders or run small business activities. It is not available to \textit{personas morales} (legal entities).\\
For a more detailed description of NIF see \vref{subsubsec:normas_de_informacion_financiera__nif_}.

    \item Normas de Contabilidad Gubernamental\\
The Normas de Contabilidad Gubernamental (NCG), or Governmental Accounting Standards, are the official framework used by government entities in Mexico to ensure transparency, accountability, and consistency in managing public funds. These standards are mandated by the Ley General de Contabilidad Gubernamental \cite{LeyGeneral}, which establishes the legal foundation for their application. The NCG consists of two main elements \cite{Conac}: the Marco Conceptual, which serves as the theoretical framework, and the Manual de Contabilidad Gubernamental, which provides practical guidance.\\
The Marco Conceptual outlines the principles, objectives, and fundamental concepts that govern public sector accounting, focusing on transparency, comparability, and accountability. Meanwhile, the Manual de Contabilidad Gubernamental acts as a practical tool for implementing these principles. It includes standardized procedures, a unified chart of accounts, and templates for financial statements and reports, ensuring consistent practices across federal, state, and municipal governments.\\
The Consejo Nacional de Armonización Contable (CONAC) oversees the harmonization of accounting practices for government entities in Mexico.

\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Which accounting standards are used by universities in Mexico?}\\
According to Prof. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo, it can be assumed that basically all universities in Mexico apply the NIF standards. One exception is National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM), the federal and public university of Mexico.

The accounting standards for the UNAM are based on the NIF framework, but an autonomous board within the university can adapt them. 

Note A.2 of the UNAM Annual Report 2023 states \cite[p.~14]{CuentaUNAM2023}:\\
\textit{“{}(…) En virtud, de que la Universidad es un organismo descentralizado del Estado, financiado principalmente mediante subsidio federal, no le es aplicable en su totalidad la normatividad referida. La Universidad aplica las políticas de información financiera específicas establecidas a través del Patronato Universitario y avaladas por la Comisión de Vigilancia Administrativa del Consejo Universitario. En consecuencia, estas políticas particulares toman el carácter de Normas de Información Financiera adecuadas a la operación y a los fines de la Universidad. (…)”{}}

Translated into English:\\
\textit{“{}(…) Given that the University is a decentralized entity of the State, primarily financed through federal subsidies, the aforementioned regulations are not fully applicable to it. The University applies specific financial information policies established by the University Board of Trustees (Patronato Universitario) and endorsed by the Administrative Oversight Commission of the University Council (Comisión de Vigilancia Administrativa del Consejo Universitario). Consequently, these particular policies take on the character of Financial Information Standards adapted to the University's operations and objectives.(…)”{}}

At UNAM, the autonomy traditionally associated with science and education appears to extend even to the governance of its financial and accounting standards.\\


Regarding IPSAS, Prof. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo is unaware of any public institution or university in Mexico that uses IPSAS for its accounting.

\subsection{A description of the two accounting standards compared}\label{subsec:a_description_of_the_two_accounting_standards_compared}
Based on the evaluation in the previous section, we have narrowed the scope to the accounting standards used by universities in the two countries. In Switzerland, these are IPSAS and Swiss GAAP FER, while in Mexico, only the NIF standards are applied. However, as outlined at the beginning of the study, the comparison focuses solely on the accounting standards used by ETH Zurich and ITESM. Although Swiss GAAP FER is not part of the direct comparison, it is included in the table overview alongside IPSAS and NIF standards, as it remains relevant for later sections of this study.

Before proceeding with the analysis, a brief description of the two accounting standards under comparison is provided in the next two sections.

\subsubsection{Normas de Información Financiera (NIF)}\label{subsubsec:normas_de_informacion_financiera__nif_}
The Normas de Información Financiera (NIF) are the Mexican accounting standards developed and published by the Consejo Mexicano de Normas de Información Financiera (CINIF). The NIF are theoretically applicable to all legal entities in Mexico, with the exception of listed companies, which are required to prepare their accounts in accordance with IFRS. Although the NIF are similar to the International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS), they are adapted to the specific economic and legal circumstances of Mexico \cite{NIFWiki}.

\textbf{Historical Development}\\
The development of the NIF reflects a decades-long evolution of accounting practices in Mexico. Initially, the Boletines de Principios de Contabilidad Generalmente Aceptados (Boletines de PCGA) served as the primary guidelines for accounting. However, with the increasing globalization of financial markets and the need for alignment with international standards, significant reforms were implemented:

\begin{itemize}
    \item 2001: The Consejo Mexicano de Normas de Información Financiera (CINIF) was established as an independent body to modernize and develop Mexico's accounting standards.

    \item 2004: The NIF officially replaced the Boletines de PCGA, marking a significant step in Mexico's accounting evolution. The transition aimed to provide a more robust and internationally aligned framework for financial reporting, enhancing transparency and comparability.

\end{itemize}

Since their introduction, the NIF have continued to evolve, reflecting changes in the global economy and the specific needs of Mexican businesses. They remain a cornerstone of financial reporting in Mexico, balancing international compatibility with local relevance \cite{JPA}.

\textbf{Componentes and Structure of NIF}\\
The NIF are structured into five main series, each addressing different aspects of financial reporting and accounting. This categorization ensures that entities have a clear and organized framework for preparing financial statements \cite{Cinif}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Series A: General Rules and Concepts\\
This series establishes the conceptual framework for financial reporting, including the objectives, principles, and qualitative characteristics of financial information. It serves as the foundation for all other standards.

    \item Series B: Presentation of Financial Statements\\
This series provides guidelines for the structure, content, and format of financial statements, such as balance sheets, income statements, and cash flow statements.

    \item Series C: Specific Accounting Standards\\
This series focuses on the recognition, measurement, and disclosure of specific financial statement items, including assets, liabilities, equity, and inventories.

    \item Series D: Revenue, Costs, and Expenses\\
Standards in this series define how revenues, costs, and expenses are recognized and presented in financial reports.

    \item Series E: Specialized Topics\\
This series addresses specific industries or unique transactions, providing guidance on areas such as agriculture, construction contracts, and other sector-specific issues.

\end{itemize}

Each series builds upon the principles defined in Series A, creating a comprehensive framework that ensures clarity, consistency, and comparability in financial reporting across various industries and entities in Mexico.

\textbf{Scope of Application}\\
NIF applies to all entities in Mexico preparing financial statements, including private companies, public entities, and those listed on the Mexican Stock Exchange. NIF is widely used across industries and regulated sectors, ensuring compliance with Mexican financial reporting requirements while aligning with global standards like IFRS where necessary \cite{Toku}.

\subsubsection{International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS)}\label{subsubsec:international_public_sector_accounting_standards__ipsas_}
The International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS) are a set of accounting standards developed by the International Public Sector Accounting Standards Board (IPSASB). They provide a framework for the preparation and presentation of financial statements for entities in the public sector.

The IPSAS aim to enhance the transparency, accountability, and comparability of financial reporting in the public sector, aligning with best practices globally. They are based on International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) issued by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB), adapted to address the specific needs of the public sector.

\textbf{Historical Development}\\
IPSAS was developed in the late 1990s by the International Federation of Accountants (IFAC) to improve transparency and accountability in public sector financial reporting. Initially based on International Accounting Standards (IAS), the first accrual-based IPSAS standards were published in 1997, addressing the unique requirements of governments and public entities. Over time, the IPSASB expanded its focus to include topics such as social benefits, taxes, and public sector assets, while aligning many standards with International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS). Today, IPSAS serves as a foundation for national frameworks in numerous countries, supporting modern financial management and comparability in the public sector \cite{IfacIpsasHistory}.

\textbf{Componentes and Structure of IPSAS}\\
IPSAS is organized into a comprehensive framework that addresses the diverse financial reporting needs of public sector entities. The standards are categorized based on their focus and purpose, ensuring a systematic approach to public sector accounting  \cite{IPSASComoponents}: 

\begin{itemize}
    \item Conceptual Framework\\
The conceptual framework provides the foundation for IPSAS by outlining the objectives and qualitative characteristics of public sector financial reporting. It defines key concepts such as accountability, decision-making usefulness, and the principles of accrual-based accounting.

    \item Accrual-Based IPSAS\\
The majority of IPSAS standards are accrual-based, focusing on the recognition, measurement, and disclosure of financial transactions and events.

    \item Cash Basis IPSAS\\
For entities that have not yet transitioned to accrual accounting, IPSAS includes a standalone Cash Basis IPSAS. This standard provides guidance on the preparation of financial statements based on cash receipts and payments, serving as a transitional step toward accrual accounting.

    \item Sector-Specific Standards\\
IPSAS addresses unique aspects of public sector accounting, such as taxation, grants, and social benefits. These standards ensure that the financial reporting needs of governments and public entities are met.

\end{itemize}

Each standard within IPSAS is designed to promote transparency, accountability, and comparability in public sector financial reporting, ensuring alignment with global best practices while addressing the specific needs of the sector.

\textbf{Scope of Application}\\
IPSAS are intended for use by public sector entities at all levels (national, regional, and local), including government ministries, departments, and agencies. Although only a minority of public sector entities fully adopt IPSAS so far, the standards serve as a foundation for accounting frameworks derived from them, such as the rule-based HRM2 (Harmonisiertes Rechnungslegungsmodell~2) in Switzerland.

\subsection{Comparison and analysis per thematical field}\label{subsec:comparison_and_analysis_per_thematical_field}
After providing an overview comparing the various standards, the subsequent sections focus on a detailed comparison of the individual areas.

\subsubsection{General Financial Reporting}\label{subsubsec:general_financial_reporting}
In this section, the appearance of the financial statements is mainly focused on, and the main differences are identified as they appear in the reporting.

As both NIF and IPSAS are based on IFRS, the structure of the balance sheet and income statement is similar. The NIF B-6 standard defines the minimum requirements for the balance sheet in Articles 52ff. As under IPSAS 1, there is also a distinction between current and non-current assets. The minimum requirements for the income statement are set out in NIF-B3 in Articles 52ff. NIF has prepared two standards for the balance sheet and income statement, whereas IPSAS has mapped everything in IPSAS 1.

Even if the basic structure of the balance sheet and income statement is identical, they appear different in practice as they address different stakeholders. Annual reports based on NIF classify expenses more likely by function (e.g., administration) or nature (e.g., material costs), whereas IPSAS often prioritizes classification by function to reflect resource allocation for public services.  Taxes play a critical role in NIF, impacting net income directly, whereas they are often irrelevant in IPSAS, as public sector entities are frequently tax-exempt. Additionally, NIF focuses on profit generation for stakeholders, while IPSAS emphasizes demonstrating how public funds are used effectively.

Furthermore, IPSAS requires the inclusion of specific items like infrastructure assets, heritage assets, and obligations related to public services, which are often less relevant under NIF. Moreover, public entities under IPSAS may handle equity in a very different way as private companies under NIF, as public resources are not “{}owned”{} but held in trust for public benefit.

Overall, NIF emphasizes financial performance, liquidity, and profitability in both the income statement and balance sheet, aiming to provide clear insights to investors and creditors. IPSAS, in contrast, focuses on public accountability, resource management, and demonstrating how public assets and liabilities are utilized for societal benefit. These differences reflect the contrasting objectives of private and public sector financial reporting.

\subsubsection{Statement of Cash Flows}\label{subsubsec:statement_of_cash_flows}
IPSAS regulates the cash flow statement in Standard 2. According to IPSAS 2.18, the inflow and outflow of cash and cash equivalents must be allocated to three main areas:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Cash flow from operating activities,

    \item Cash flow from investing activities, and

    \item Cash flow from financing activities.

\end{itemize}

In principle, cash flow from operating activities can be determined both directly and indirectly, whereby the IPSASB recommends the use of the direct method (see \cite[p.~63]{IPSASPraxisHB}).

In the NIF standards, the cash flow statement is covered in standard NIF B-2. Article 15 also defines the three above-mentioned categories under a), b) and d). d) of the same article is repealed and e) to h) describe the net change and holdings at the beginning and end (serves also as a check) as well as effects due to changes in value (e.g. due to exchange rate changes). Furthermore, the direct method for calculating the operating cash flow is defined in Article 44ff and the indirect method in Article 48ff.

In summary, it can be said that the cash flow statement is essentially identical in both accounting standards.

\subsubsection{Revenue Recognition}\label{subsubsec:revenue_recognition}
There are major deviations in the area of revenue recognition standards. The main reason for this is that NIF D-1, unlike IPSAS 9, 11 and 23, is based on IFRS 15 (see Article IN16 of NIF D-1).

With the replacement of the previous IPSAS standards for revenue recognition and the upcoming introduction of IPSAS 47, the situation will change, as IPSAS 47 is also based on IFRS 15.

This will reduce the differences between IPSAS and NIF. However, as revenue recognition transactions in the public sector are fundamentally different from those in the private sector (particularly due to non-exchange transactions), IPSAS 47 will continue to contain specific rules that differ from NIF-D1 and IFRS 15. This is further text that is not really worthful.

\subsubsection{Tangible Assets}\label{subsubsec:tangible_assets}
IPSAS regulates property, plant and equipment in Standard 17. Initial measurement must be based on acquisition or production cost. In the context of subsequent measurement (IPSAS 17.42), a public entity can choose either the cost model with reference to cost (IPSAS 17.43) or the revaluation model (IPSAS 17.44)(see \cite[p.~254]{IPSASPraxisHB}).

As with IPSAS, acquisition or production costs are also relevant for initial measurement under NIF C-6. However, there is no option for subsequent measurement and the cost model must be used (see Article 46.1.1 of NIF C-6). The situation is different for investment property: According to NIF C-17, it is possible to choose between the cost model and the revaluation model for subsequent measurement (see Article IN16 of NIF-C17). This means that there is no deviation from IPSAS 16 with regard to the subsequent measurement of investment property.

When examining the regulations for inventories, it becomes clear that the principles applied under different accounting standards vary depending on the context and sector they are designed for. Although IPSAS 12 and NIF C-4 are both based on IAS 2 from IFRS and apply the same fundamental principles for inventory accounting, there are notable differences between the two standards. These differences primarily arise from the distinct contexts in which they are applied. IPSAS 12, tailored for the public sector, often deals with the inventory of goods that are not typically encountered in private-sector accounting under NIF C-4. For instance, IPSAS 12.14A highlights military inventories, including single-use items such as ammunition, missiles, rockets, and bombs, which are delivered by weapons systems. Such items are specific to public-sector entities and demonstrate the broader scope of IPSAS 12 compared to NIF C-4, which focuses more on traditional commercial inventories like goods held for sale or production.

\subsubsection{Intangible Assets}\label{subsubsec:intangible_assets}
IPSAS 31 defines which intangible assets can be capitalized and how they are measured. According to IPSAS 31.52, costs incurred during the research phase of a project cannot be capitalized as an intangible asset. The reason for this is that the potential for future economic benefits or service potential is uncertain at this stage. Research costs, including activities to gain new knowledge or explore alternatives, must be expensed as incurred.

In contrast, the costs for the development phase can be capitalized, but only if certain strict criteria are met. These criteria include evidence of the technical feasibility of completing the asset, the intention and ability to complete, evidence of probable future economic benefits or service potential, sufficient resources to complete the project and the ability to measure reliably the costs associated with the development phase. Examples of development activities that may meet these criteria are the design and construction of a prototype or the testing of a new product.

NIF C-8 applies the same criteria as IPSAS 31, as both standards are based on IAS 38. This harmonization ensures a uniform treatment of research and development costs in the international and Mexican accounting frameworks. Research costs are always expensed, while development costs can only be recognized as intangible assets if the conditions described are met. This approach underlines the importance of distinguishing between these phases in order to maintain the transparency and accuracy of financial reporting.

\subsubsection{Impairment}\label{subsubsec:impairment}
In relation to impairment, a specific aspect of IPSAS is that a separate standard has been created for the impairment of non-cash-generating assets. What does non-cash-generating assets mean?

Many assets owned by public entities do not generate significant direct cash flows or income. Instead, they primarily serve to achieve (politically) defined objectives. This is especially true for key infrastructure assets in the public sector, such as non-toll roads, bridges, or public buildings. These assets are typically available to the general public, largely free of charge, and are therefore considered unprofitable from a business perspective(see \cite[p.~345]{IPSASPraxisHB}).

It is evident that in the context of the (subsequent) valuation of assets held solely to fulfill specific objectives, the same accounting rules cannot be applied as those used for commercially held goods intended to achieve economic success. For this reason, the IPSAS Board has created Standard 21 to take account of the characteristics of these assets.

In addition to this standard, there is also IPSAS 26, which deals with cash-generating assets. This standard applies the same concept as the corresponding NIF C-15 standard, that just covers cash-generating assets.

\subsubsection{Provisions and Liabilities}\label{subsubsec:provisions_and_liabilities}
IPSAS 19 and NIF C-9 are based on IAS 37 and therefore use the same basic principles. An important common principle is that, in contrast to the rules of the Swiss Code of Obligations (CO), provisions for expenses in the sense of internal obligations are not permitted.

Another similarity between IPSAS and NIF is that, in addition to legal obligations, constructive obligations are also sufficient for the recognition of a provision. A constructive obligation that leads to a provision arises when an entity, through its actions or established practices, creates a valid expectation in third parties that it will fulfill a commitment, making it practically unavoidable.

\subsubsection{Consolidation and Group Reporting}\label{subsubsec:consolidation_and_group_reporting}
The differences in how control is defined between IPSAS and NIF stem from the distinct nature of the public and private sectors. In the public sector, parent and subsidiary entities are often organized around legal mandates and the provision of public services, whereas in the private sector, these relationships are primarily based on ownership structures and economic interests.

According to IPSAS, control exists when an entity has the power to direct relevant activities, regardless of ownership, often through legal authority or the appointment of key decision makers to governance bodies. The focus is on the provision of public services, and neither financial benefits nor ownership interests are required to exercise control. In contrast, NIF defines control primarily through majority ownership or contractual rights that ensure that financial and operational decisions are aligned with economic interests. The NIF approach emphasizes control on the basis of financial returns, whereby influence by the management bodies alone is insufficient without economic benefits.

\subsubsection{Leases}\label{subsubsec:leases}
IPSAS 13 for leases was to be applied until the end of 2024. This was based on the distinction between operating and finance leases. With the new IPSAS 43, which came into force on 1 January 2025, the Rigth-of-Use model (ROU) is now applied. IPSAS 43 is derived from IFRS 16, which has already prescribed the ROU model since January in 2019.

As NIF D-5 is also derived from IFRS 16, the same principles are applied as for IPSAS 43 and the ROU model also applies. This means that IPSAS and NIF apply similar principles regarding the overall treatment of leases.

\subsubsection{Financial Instruments}\label{subsubsec:financial_instruments}
The IPSAS, like the NIF standards, are based on IFRS 9, which came into force on January in 2018.

One of the significant achievements of IFRS 9 is the adoption of the ECL (Expected Credit Loss). This model has been incorporated into IPSAS 41 for the public sector and the NIF C-16 standards for the private sector in Mexico. It represents a fundamental shift toward more proactive and forward-looking financial reporting, enabling entities to recognize credit losses based on expected future performance rather than waiting for evidence of impairment.

All three accounting standards use a similar three-step model for the application of the ECL model:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Stage 1 applies to assets with no significant increase in credit risk, where 12-month expected credit losses are calculated.

    \item Stage 2 applies to assets with a significant increase in credit risk, requiring the calculation of lifetime expected credit losses.

    \item Stage 3 is reserved for credit-impaired assets, also focusing on lifetime losses but with adjustments for credit defaults.

\end{itemize}

In addition to the three-stage model, all three standards include a simplified approach for specific types of financial assets, such as trade receivables, lease receivables, and contractual assets. The simplified approach streamlines the process by bypassing the need to monitor credit risk changes and instead directly calculates lifetime expected credit losses. This approach is particularly useful for short-term or low-risk assets and helps entities reduce complexity while maintaining compliance with the standards.

\subsubsection{Employee Benefits}\label{subsubsec:employee_benefits}
The classification of employee benefits in IPSAS 39 and NIF D-3, when focusing solely on the structure, is closely aligned with the IFRS standard IAS 19 upon which they are based, as all three categorize benefits into four main types: short-term benefits, post-employment benefits, other long-term benefits, and termination benefits. In IPSAS 39, these four categories are addressed in the following paragraphs: short-term benefits (Articles 9-25), post-employment benefits (Articles 26-154), other long-term benefits (Articles 155-161), and termination benefits (Articles 162-174). Similarly, NIF D-3 adopts this structure with detailed provisions for short-term benefits (Articles 42.1,1-42.4.11), long-term benefits (Articles 43.1.1-43.3.5), termination benefits (Articles 44.1.1-44.2.6), and post-employment benefits (Articles 45.1.1-45.8.2). This alignment reflects the influence of IAS 19, ensuring consistency in the recognition, measurement, and disclosure of employee benefits across these standards.

From a material perspective, IPSAS 39 introduces several distinctions compared to IAS 19. IPSAS~39 provides additional guidance on public sector bonus plans. For discounting post-employment obligations, IAS 19 requires the use of a discount rate based on high-quality corporate bond yields, whereas IPSAS 39 mandates a rate reflecting the time value of money and requires disclosure of the basis for determining the rate. IPSAS 39 introduces a rebuttable presumption that long-term disability payments are generally less uncertain than post-employment benefits, a feature absent in IAS 19. Remeasurements of defined benefit liabilities (assets) are recognized in net assets/equity under IPSAS 39, whereas IAS 19 recognizes them in other comprehensive income. Additionally, IPSAS 39 uses different terminology, such as “{}revenue”{} instead of “{}income”{} and “{}controlled entities”{} instead of “{}subsidiaries.”{} (see \cite[p.~20]{Eurostat}).

NIF D-3 is also derived from IAS 19, but the differences are more limited according to Prof. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo, as the target audience of the NIF standards in the private sector aligns more closely with that of IFRS.

\subsubsection{Specific Transactions and Topics}\label{subsubsec:specific_transactions_and_topics}
When we compare the standards in this section, it becomes clear that the different accounting standards are designed for the specific needs of public entities and private companies. Each framework focuses on the requirements of its audience, leading to differences in how certain topics are addressed.

For example, IPSAS 10 Financial Reporting in Hyperinflationary Economies helps public sector entities deal with the challenges of hyperinflation. A similar standard exists in the private sector with NIF B-10: Inflation Effects, which addresses inflation accounting for businesses. Another example is IPSAS 32: Service Concession Arrangements: Grantor, which provides guidance for public sector contracts involving private operators, compared to INIF 17 Service Concession Contract, which focuses on these contracts from the private sector perspective.

However, some topics are specific to the public sector and have no equivalent in other frameworks. For instance, IPSAS 42 Social Benefits sets out how governments should account for obligations like pensions and unemployment benefits. There is no comparable standard in NIF or FER, as these frameworks do not cover public welfare programs.

This shows that while there are overlaps between frameworks, some standards are unique to the sector they are designed for, reflecting the different objectives of public entities and private companies.

\subsection{Conclusions from the comparative analysis}\label{subsec:conclusions_from_the_comparative_analysis}
The comparative analysis highlights the distinct focus and objectives of the accounting frameworks studied. While IPSAS caters to the public sector with an emphasis on transparency, accountability, and effective resource management, NIF is tailored to private companies, prioritizing profitability, liquidity, and stakeholder returns. Despite their differing goals, both frameworks are heavily based on and strongly aligned with IFRS, resulting in significant overlaps in fundamental concepts and principles.

For instance, areas like financial instruments, impairment, and employee benefits showcase significant alignment due to their shared basis in IFRS standards. However, unique challenges faced by the public sector, such as accounting for non-cash-generating assets (IPSAS 21) or social benefits (IPSAS 42), are addressed exclusively in IPSAS, reflecting its focus on public service delivery rather than economic gain. Similarly, NIF places greater emphasis on tax implications and profit generation, which are often irrelevant in the context of public sector accounting.

The analysis also revealed how each framework adapts to its audience’s needs, such as the differences in segment reporting (IPSAS 18 vs. NIF B-5), where IPSAS prioritizes function-based reporting to reflect resource allocation, and NIF offers flexibility for classification by function or nature. Additionally, the introduction of IPSAS 47, based on IFRS 15 for revenue recognition, signals a continued effort to harmonize public sector accounting with private sector standards, while maintaining sector-specific rules for non-exchange transactions.

In conclusion, the comparison underscores the adaptability of accounting standards to their respective environments. While certain standards are universal, others are tailored to address sector-specific priorities, ensuring both public accountability and private sector profitability are effectively supported.

\section{Key takeaways and personal insights from the comparison}\label{sec:key_takeaways_and_personal_insights_from_the_comparison}
First of all, after the conversations I had with the representatives of ITESM, I was impressed by the great competence and open manner in which they explained the Mexican NIF standards to me. Furthermore, I was not aware that Mexican accounting standards are so sophisticated and comprehensive. As the comparative study has shown, they are largely based on IFRS standards like IPSAS.

Due to the complexity of these accounting standards, I was surprised by Prof. Noemí Vásquez Quevedo statement that the NIF standards are valid for all legal entities in Mexico. She assured me that there is no simplification for smaller SMEs, such as under Swiss GAAP FER with the six core standards. When I asked how smaller SMEs deal with the introduction of such extensive regulations and the resulting complexity in the financial reporting, she said that not all legal entities follow or apply these standards fully or to the same extent. This is where cultural differences between Mexico and Switzerland became apparent. In Switzerland, Swiss GAAP FER specifies which regulations apply to which size of company and this is usually implemented. It would be interesting to find out to what extent materiality plays a role in the audit of Mexican companies in the assessment of deviations, but this was not part of this study.

And what is the situation regarding materiality under IPSAS? Does the principle of materiality go so far that individual requirements defined in the standards do not have to be applied?

The IPSAS Board interprets its own regulations in this regard as follows \cite[p.~1]{Materiality}:

\textit{“{}Put simply, the concept of materiality means that specific accounting requirements }\textit{\textbf{can be disregarded for insignificant matters}}\textit{. However, these specific accounting requirements must be followed in full for important matters. Determining whether a matter is insignificant or not depends on a number of factors--the nature of the matter or item; its size; and the particular circumstances of each entity. Materiality applies to items individually and collectively.”{}}

The text marked in bold indicates that this is possible under certain circumstances. But it is clear that the further you push the boundaries in this respect, the more uncertain the terrain becomes and the more likely you are to provoke discussions with the auditors. What adds to the challenge is the inherently subjective nature of materiality assessments and that the accounting sector often has a risk-averse working culture.

But ultimately, financial reporting and the information reported therein are not an end in themselves, but serve the purpose of accountability and decision-making (see \cite[Article 2.1, p.~12]{IPSASFW}). The 'Conceptual Framework For General Purpose Financial Reporting By Public Sector Entities' mentions this explicitly in \cite[Article 2.2, p.~12]{IPSASFW}:

\textit{“{}Financial reporting is not an end in itself. Its purpose is to provide information useful to users of GPFRs. The objectives of financial reporting are therefore determined by reference to the users of GPFRs, and their information needs.”{}}

In my opinion, in addition to regularity and compliance with the law, decision-makers should place greater emphasis on the economic efficiency of financial reporting, particularly by giving more consideration to the aspect of materiality. This is especially important given the strained financial situation of the federal government and public finances in general.
